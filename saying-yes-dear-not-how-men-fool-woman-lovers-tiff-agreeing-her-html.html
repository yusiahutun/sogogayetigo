Simply agreeing with women in a bid to avoid conflict does not work, according to a new study.

Researchers from The University of Arizona assessed the communication styles of 44 heterosexual couples focusing on the differences between the sexes.

They concluded that girlfriends and wives picked up on the fact that their partner's 'yes dears' and 'uh-huhs' were disingenuous, which in turn aggravated the situation.

Breakdown in communication: Men do not fool women when they agree with them, according to a new study

A 2011 study by Creative Conflict Resolutions, which involved more than 900 men, found that 68per cent preferred to say 'yes dear' to temper their other halves.

Neil Warner, who headed up the research, said as well as being insincere this passive aggressive behavior is confusing.

'The partner of a passive aggressive person can spend the best 20-30 years of their life trying to decode this maddening double message,' he said.

'Meanwhile, in trying to decode it, the innocent partner can be accused of being crazy, aggressive and overly demanding.'

Indeed, researchers behind the recent study by the University of Arizona, say that unconvinced by their boyfriends or husband's remarks, women will keep nagging until a 'mutually satisfying resolution' is reached.

Ashley Randall, post-doctoral fellow, explained: 'If you think about a couple that is trying to cooperate with one another, the man might go along and say, "oh sure, honey, this is great, are we almost done?"

'Whereas the women might say, "I’m so glad that you’re happy, but I just want to talk about this one other thing because I think we’re really getting at a resolution."

'Cooperation is something that's invaluable and instrumental in a successful relationship,' he added.

The study was published in Journal of Social and Personal Relationships and featured in the journal’s podcast series, Relationship Matters.

Social psychology literature on cooperation tells us that women generally tend to cooperate more, while men often try to avoid conflict.

'This research provides another avenue to understanding how partners’ emotions can become linked,’ Mr Randall said, adding, ’But future research is needed on how these emotional patterns may ultimately contribute to the longevity, or demise, of the romantic relationship.'

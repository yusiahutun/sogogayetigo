Man who was sexually abused by his dad finally has him jailed

February 2024 Â· 6 minute read

A man who was sexually abused by his father as a boy has revealed how he finally found the courage to report him to the police after living in shame and fear for years.

Gareth, now 36, from Yorkshire, was just seven-years-old when the abuse started.

His parents had divorced when he was a toddler and his father often molested him when he stayed with him at weekends.

Gareth was only seven-years-old when his father started sexually abusing him and he was too young to understand what was happening (file photo)

'I was seven years old the first time it happened. I was sleeping in my father's bed and he started touching me, I was so scared I didn't know what to do,' Gareth - not his real name - recalls.

'He was my father and I thought that whatever he was doing to me was normal, I didn't know any different. He made me feel really dirty. I was too young to understand that it was him being the bad person, not me. 

'The abuse would happen at his house when I visited, not every weekend, but most weekends.'

Gareth didn't feel close to his mother so felt unable to confide in her and also had a tough time when he was under her roof. 

He explains: 'My mum was in and out of relationships so her boyfriends were always round at the house. Some of them were violent and really nasty with me. 

'One of them would lock me in the bathroom when he wanted to go out to bingo with my mum. I didn't feel cared for.

'We moved around a lot and the houses we lived in were really dirty. Mum would always make sure there was food on the table but I never had clean clothes. I learnt at an early age how to look after myself.'

Scared and alone, Gareth felt he had no one to turn to and so the sexual abuse at the hands of his father continued into his teens.

The abuse stopped when Gareth was 13 but he continued to struggle with what had happened to him. He finally went to the police when he was in his thirties (file photo)

He said: 'I didn't tell anyone what was happening to me. I thought about speaking to a teacher but I was really scared - my father had a really bad temper and was a threatening man. 

'I tried to ignore what my father was doing and get on with my life. I was always a quiet child, and even now I don't speak a lot. I internalised my feelings and worries about my life and the abuse and locked my thoughts in a box in my head.'

Gareth said the sexual abuse finally stopped when he was 13 but it continued to impact on his life.

'It affected relationships I had as I grew older. Sex used to really worry me and I would delay it within the relationship for as long as possible. The thought of having sex really frightened me, and I didn't feel like I could tell my girlfriends at the time why,' he said. 

'I used to get wound up, angry and moody about things. I think because I kept everything inside about what had happened to me and didn't cry or talk about it sometimes my feelings would just burst out of me and I couldnât control them.'

It was only after becoming a father himself that Gareth realised how terrible his parent's crimes were and that he couldn't let him get away with them.

He said: 'About four years ago I decided to go to the police and the case went to court. My father initially pleaded not guilty to the offences. I didn't want the case to go to trial; I just wanted him to admit what he'd done.'

Gareth wasn't his father's only victim and as the police investigated, he was charged with abusing another two boys.

He eventually admitted what he had done and pleaded guilty. Jailing him for 13 years in 2013, a judge called him wicked and evil. 

Now in his early sixties, he must register as a sex offender for the rest of his life. 

Speaking of the prosecution, Gareth said: 'When my father was convicted and sentenced I felt free for the first time in my life. I didn't feel like I was looking over my shoulder anymore.

'As a father to young children myself, I was always fearful that he would find a way to get to them too. I know I can take my children to the local park and that he won't be there, he's going to be locked away.'

Now Gareth wants to help other children in need so he's signed up to run next year's Virgin Money London Marathon for the NSPCC. 

He said: 'When I had my children, I realised how much my father had ruined my childhood. I don't want them to have to suffer anything like I did. I just want to give them everything that we didn't have as kids.

'I decided to run for the NSPCC because I want to protect other children from going through the awful abuse I did as a child. 

'I want to promote the NSPCC's amazing work protecting children and preventing abuse.' 

Gareth agreed to share his story to promote the work of the NSPCC and he hopes other people will sign up to run the London Marathon for them and raise lots of money in the process.

He said the training has been beneficial for both his physical and mental health.  

The NSPCC are looking for more volunteers to run and raise money for them in next year's marathon

'I've found the training really therapeutic, I actually really enjoy running now and have lost over three and a half stone. 

'It feels like therapy for me, I can go out and work thoughts and feelings through my head and I come back feeling better, like I've let go of what happened to me a little.

'I'd like to say to anyone out there who is thinking of running for the NSPCC to just do it. 

'They help so many children facing such awful abuse. You'd be helping children with childhoods like mine get support and care when they need it.'

The NSPCC has been announced as the official charity partner for the Virgin Money London Marathon 2016 - a year that also marks 30 years of the charity's ChildLine service.

NSPCC chief executive, Peter Wanless, said: '2016 is shaping up to be a huge year for the NSPCC and, in particular, our ChildLine service which will be marking 30 years. 

'It's fantastic that in this milestone year, we've been chosen to be the official charity of the Virgin Money London Marathon, and we're calling on runners across the UK to join Team NSPCC to make it our biggest and best team ever!'

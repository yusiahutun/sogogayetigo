Can Art Save the World?

May 2024 · 5 minute read

How do we get people who haven’t been directly or substantially impacted by climate change to care about mitigating it? While there is no shortage of scientific reports and statistics outlining the growing environmental crisis, that kind of evidence falls short in its ability to make a human connection with readers and generate empathy for others—which is where the power of art comes in.

First launched four years ago, #CreateCOP is an annual open call for artists between the ages of 14 and 30 to submit work (in any medium) that responds to—and encourages dialogue about—climate-related issues. Pegged to the annual United Nations Climate Change Conference of the Parties, which this year took place in Dubai, capital of the United Arab Emirates from November 30 to December 13, the competition is presented by global creative agency Art Partner and presided over by a panel of judges including photographer Mario Sorrenti, sustainable fashion advocate Marina Testino, and Atmos magazine editor in chief Willow Defebaugh, among others.

“What captivates and inspires me most is how personal these artworks are.” —Marina Testino

“Art is a universal language—it has a unique ability to not only shed light on the impacts of the climate crisis, but also to present solutions in a compelling and accessible manner,” Testino says.

“It’s a crisis that must speak to our humanity,” adds Defebaugh, “and that’s what the arts have always been for.”

This year, #CreateCOP28 received submissions from over 89 countries, with eight finalists awarded cash prizes of up to $10,000. Ranging from dioramas and AI-generated imagery to film and photography, the projects commented on everything from the life cycles of various textiles to the impact of global warming on weather through diverse, deeply considered points of view.

“What captivates and inspires me most is how personal these artworks are,” says Testino. “Despite the anxieties that these artists inevitably face, their dedication to the creative process and their courage to share their stories convey a sense of optimism that I deeply admire.” All of the finalists’ work, as well as the pieces that received honorable mentions, can be viewed online in a digital gallery space.

Filipino photographer Gab Mejia took home the top prize this year, for his intensely personal film The Passage of Storms. Highlighting the physical and emotional aftermath of the super typhoons that frequently hit his home country, it features heart-wrenching anecdotes about the struggles and resilience of fishermen and farmers in the Philippines, allowing the audience to see the devastation on a human level. As Mejia’s film underscores, climate change disproportionately impacts underserved coastal communities, which are often the least able to prepare for and recover from flooding, heat waves, and other disastrous weather events—despite the fact that these communities are typically among the parties least responsible for human-produced climate change.

New York City–based Swiss artist Julia Daser seemed to be responding to that irony with her project Flooded House, which asked audience members to consider how the choices they made in their everyday lives contributed to the larger problems of CO₂ emissions and climate change. To do this, she created a miniature replica of her studio apartment, which was connected to a game that let users make decisions about how to spend the day in the space. Those choices encompassed everything from whether to turn on lights to how much water to use and what food to eat; based on these factors, the studio apartment then filled with water accordingly, the amount of flooding directly proportional to how their choices would contribute to the rising of Earth’s ocean. “I believe that we already have enough technical solutions to mitigate climate change—if we wanted to, we could live more sustainably starting tomorrow,” Daser says. “Change first starts with people’s minds. Our strongest weapons against climate change are empathy for us and the planet, self-reflection on our actions, and willingness to act.”

Corinne Rivera, the American artist behind the AI-generated series Styles of the Anthropocene, harnessed the allure of high fashion and the power of tech and influencer culture to draw attention to various climate catastrophes, like algae blooms, coral bleaching, and oil spills. “I love creating new ways for people to engage with our climate crisis,” she explains. “[My project] poses the question: What if we paid as much attention to climate issues as we did influencers?” By anthropomorphizing such climate issues, and representing them with glamorous, digitally generated avatars, Rivera hopes to get more people to engage with news and information about the environmental crisis, and would like to eventually use them to promote climate-friendly nongovernmental organizations or technologies and tools such as biochar, regenerative kelp, and direct air capture.

“I hope people who view this work will be similarly moved to change how they see the world around them.” —Willow Defebaugh

While both Daser and Rivera’s projects propose actionable climate solutions, Chloe Karnezi’s video Junkspace wants consumers to reconsider waste, in terms of both how we think of it and our failure to transform it. “The project explores today’s consumer culture and takes inspiration from a quote by author John Scanlan: ‘Garbage is what remains when the good, fruitful, valuable, nourishing, and useful has been taken,’” Karnezi explains. After picking up trash around her native Athens, Greece, and scanning it using photogrammetry, she built a digital “sculpture,” creating a “speculative limbo” where trash resides. “I examine waste as a failure of the imagination. If garbage starts where usefulness ends, it is up to each of us to reimagine use cases for objects, thus prolonging their life cycle and postponing their transformation into waste.”

Says Defebaugh: “Junkspace prompted me to wonder, What if everything we wasted had a voice? It challenged me to look at what some might consider to be empty and meaningless as anything but. I hope people who view this work will be similarly moved to change how they see the world around them. That’s what art does.”

Ariana Marsh is Harper Bazaar’s senior features editor. Working across print and digital, she covers the arts, culture, fashion, literature, and entertainment—and a bit of everything in between.
